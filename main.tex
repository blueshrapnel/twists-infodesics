\begin{abstract}
% DRAFT — one paragraph, to be rewritten once results are in.
A twist relabels an agent's actions per state.  Optimised twists build
\emph{habit cycles}: attractors that a single repeated action label drains
the environment into.  A companion paper characterises these structures
empirically; this paper explains them.  We show that a repeated-label flow
is a pure \emph{infodesic} in the space-of-goals geometry --- a route
followable with no state information --- and that the asymmetric homing
mode is a \emph{cost infodesic} segmented at informationally salient
waypoints: the free-energy triangle inequality fails across goal-specific
policies, policy switching becomes advantageous, and the doorway states at
which switches occur are exactly where the evolved label vocabulary changes
hands.  Curvature, concentrated at walls, is the knob: where the world is
flat the pure infodesic, the value geodesic, and the cost infodesic
coincide and homing saturates; where walls concentrate curvature the desics
split, routes segment into single-label legs, and the home-plus-escape
vocabulary of the companion paper emerges.  Switching costs quantify the
segmentation: the cost of abandoning a habit decomposes into an ambient
term set by the environment's drift and a dominant policy-specific term set
by the habit being abandoned.
\end{abstract}

\section{Background: the free-energy geometry and its desics}
\label{sec:background}

% TODO: reconcile notation with cognitive-geometry.tex §Infodesics before
% submission; definitions below are the working forms.

For an informationally bounded agent, the cost of reaching a goal $g$ from
a state $s$ is the free energy of the goal-optimal policy,
$\Fen_g(s) = -V^{\pi^*}(s) + \beta^{-1} \ItgD^{\pi^*}(s)$: expected path
cost plus the decision information the route demands, weighed by the
agent's parsimony $\beta$.  Collecting these values over all ordered pairs
gives a cost matrix $D[s, g] = \Fen_g(s)$ --- a quasi-metric on the world:
non-negative, zero on the diagonal, and in general \emph{asymmetric},
because moving with a prevailing structure costs less information than
moving against it.

Routes through this geometry come in kinds.  A \emph{pure infodesic} is a
route followable with no state information at all: action selection
independent of state, $\ItgD \to 0$ --- following the same command
everywhere, the informational analogue of a loxodrome's constant bearing.
A \emph{value geodesic} is a shortest route in the expected-cost sense
alone.  A \emph{cost infodesic} is a route optimal in the full free
energy; on such a route through an intermediate $B$, the triangle
inequality
\[
  D[A, C] \;\leq\; D[A, B] + D[B, C]
\]
holds with equality.  Where the triangle inequality \emph{fails} ---
$D[A, B] + D[B, C] < D[A, C]$ --- reaching $C$ via $B$, abandoning the
goal-$C$ policy at $B$ for a fresh one, is strictly cheaper than the
direct route: multi-policy processing beats single-policy processing, and
the optimal behaviour \emph{segments} at $B$.  The states at which such
segmentation is profitable are the geometry's informationally salient
waypoints.

On a flat, translation-invariant world these three notions collapse: one
constant command is also shortest and also free-energy-optimal, and no
segmentation ever pays.  Where structure --- walls, doorways, seams ---
concentrates curvature, the desics separate, and the question of this
paper is what evolution does about it when it is allowed to redesign the
action labels themselves.

\section{Single-label flows are pure infodesics}
\label{sec:pure}

A pure infodesic is a sequence visitable with no state information ---
action selection independent of state, decision information
$\ItgD \to 0$: following the same direction with as little cognitive
correction as possible.  A single-label flow is exactly that.  Press one
label in every state: the label is state-independent even though the twist
makes the \emph{physical} direction vary cell to cell.  The home vector of
the companion paper is the direction field of a pure infodesic; the habit
cycle is the attractor the pure infodesic drains into --- its terminus.
The habit is the whole pure-infodesic object: flow plus limit set.

This identification does real work.  It explains why decision-information
descent builds habit cycles at all: the fitness rewards routes that
approach pure infodesics, and under a twist the set of realisable pure
infodesics is exactly the set of single-label flows --- so evolution
shapes $\sigma$ until some label's flow is worth following from almost
anywhere.  It also assigns the correct formal role to the attractor: the
cycle is not a defect of the flow but its goal, the recurrent set the
open-loop policy is \emph{for}.

\section{The asymmetric mode is a segmented cost infodesic}
\label{sec:segmentation}

\paragraph{Segmentation is a walled-world phenomenon.}  We measured the
triangle inequality systematically: for each environment and
$\beta \in \{0.3, 1, 3\}$, every ordered goal triple $(A, B, C)$ of the
Cartesian world, a violation being a strictly cheaper segmented route
(Figure~\ref{fig:triangle}).  The dichotomy is clean.  Flat worlds ---
the torus and the bounded open grid --- produce \emph{zero} violating
triples at every $\beta$: the maximum gap is strictly negative, no policy
switch ever pays, and the desics coincide as Section~\ref{sec:background}
predicts.  Walled worlds violate: on \code{four\_rooms} one in six
ordered pairs has a profitable midpoint; the corridor ring violates on
$3$--$4\%$ of triples.  The gap is informational in origin: its maximum
grows as $\beta$ falls ($1.1 \to 3.4 \to 11.0$ bits-equivalent across
$\beta = 3 \to 1 \to 0.3$ on \code{four\_rooms}) --- value alone
satisfies the triangle inequality, and it is the information cost of one
policy serving a two-legged route that makes segmentation pay, amplified
by parsimony.

\paragraph{The waypoints are the doorway flanks --- and behaviour
agrees.}  The profitable midpoints do not sit \emph{on} the doorway
cells but one step past them.  The behavioural face of the same fact:
rolling greedy routes for every start--goal pair and recording where the
issued label changes, the switch rate per visit is exactly zero at the
doorway cells under the Cartesian labelling and maximal on their flanks
($0.46$ against $0.38$ elsewhere) --- the flow passes \emph{through} a
door; the decision happens after crossing (Figure~\ref{fig:switch}).
Geometry predicts behaviour: per-state switch events correlate with
profitable-midpoint counts at $r = 0.72$.  The flat control switches
\emph{more} overall ($0.47$ per step against $0.37$) but with no spatial
structure: plenty of switching, no waypoints.  Segmentation, on both the
geometric and the behavioural ledger, is what walls make.

\paragraph{What the twist does to the geometry --- the refuted
prediction.}  If the evolved twist internalises route structure, one
might expect the twisted world's geometry to offer \emph{fewer}
profitable segmentation points.  The opposite holds
(Figure~\ref{fig:twist-triangle}): on the exemplar twist the violating
pairs multiply from $258$ to $610$ of $1{,}560$, at every $\beta$, with
larger gaps --- while the mean direct cost falls.  The twist does not
flatten the cost geometry; it \emph{curves it further}.  The labelling
installs a strong drift field --- the homing flow --- and a directional
geometry is precisely one in which two-leg routes, riding one flow and
then another, undercut direct ones.  There is no tension with the
behavioural switch reduction above: the optimal policies issue fewer
switch \emph{events} because each label now serves a longer leg, while
the geometry affords more profitable re-planning points because the
flows are stronger.  Behaviour segments less; geometry affords more.
Evolution does not straighten the free-energy geometry --- it bends it
so that its own habit legs are the cheap segments, which is what it
means, in geometric terms, for the representation to have absorbed the
segmentation.

\section{Switching costs quantify the segmentation}
\label{sec:switching}

Section~\ref{sec:segmentation} located where segmentation pays; this
section prices the switch.  The Space-of-Goals programme
\citep{Archer2022-space-of-goals} left exactly this as its central open
item: a conjectured \emph{relaxed} triangle inequality
$\Fen_g(s) \leq \Fen_{s'}(s) + \Fen_g(s') + C_{\mathrm{switch}}$, with
$C_{\mathrm{switch}}$ read as a \emph{disambiguation} cost --- zero when
the two legs' trajectory supports do not overlap, and otherwise the
entropy of section membership accumulated over the shared states.  We
tested that reading and refuted it; the corrected theory is stated below
and validated to numerical precision.

In plain terms, the finding is this.  Every route pursued efficiently
under a decision budget settles into a \emph{habitual mix of moves} ---
mostly-east, say, with occasional corrections --- and it is deviations
from that mix, not the moves themselves, that cost decisions.  The
direct journey must run on one mix; a journey split at a waypoint runs
each leg on its own.  Wherever the two legs want \emph{different} mixes,
the split journey is cheaper than the direct one --- the way a two-leg
air fare can undercut the direct flight
(Figure~\ref{fig:stopover}).  The 2022 conjecture priced the split as
the cost of \emph{remembering which leg you are on}.  The experiments
below show that this is the wrong ledger: remembering turns out to be
nearly free, and \emph{changing vocabulary} is what the discount
actually pays for.  Nothing in this reading softens the formal result
--- the discount is exactly the free-energy triangle violation, and the
vocabulary change is exactly the difference between the legs' action
marginals.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{F-stopover-schematic.png}
  \caption[Why a stopover can beat the direct route (schematic)]{Why a
  stopover can beat the direct route.  A journey run directly must use
  one habitual mix of moves throughout (\emph{left}); split at a
  waypoint, each leg runs on its own mix (\emph{middle}).  Where the
  two legs want different mixes, the split journey is cheaper, and the
  discount is exactly the value of changing vocabulary at the waypoint
  (\emph{right}; illustrative numbers --- the measured version is
  Figure~\ref{fig:decomposition}).}
  \label{fig:stopover}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Setup.}  For a memoryless policy $\pi$ and a
\emph{state-independent} reference distribution $q$ over actions, write
\[
  \Fen^{\pi, q}_g(s) \;=\;
  \mathbb{E}^{\pi}_s \Big[ \textstyle\sum_{t < \tau_g}
  \big( -r_t + \tfrac{1}{\beta}
  \log_2 \tfrac{\pi(a_t \mid s_t)}{q(a_t)} \big) \Big].
\]
The free energy of Section~\ref{sec:background} is the self-consistent
object $\Fen_g = \Fen^{\pi_g, \hat{p}_g}_g$, with
$(\pi_g, \hat{p}_g)$ the Blahut--Arimoto fixed point: each goal's
optimal policy is priced against its \emph{own} converged action
marginal.  Two facts carry the section.  First, for fixed $\pi$ the
reference minimising $\Fen^{\pi,q}$ is the route's visit-weighted action
marginal $\rho(a) = n^{-1} \sum_x \nu(x)\, \pi(a \mid x)$, where
$\nu(x)$ counts expected decisions at $x$ and $n = \sum_x \nu(x)$ is the
expected route length.  Second, swapping the reference has an
\emph{exact} price --- no bound, an identity:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta(q) \;\defeq\; \Fen^{\pi, q} - \Fen^{\pi, \rho}
  \;=\; \tfrac{n}{\beta}\, D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( \rho \,\middle\|\, q \right).
  \label{eq:prior-swap}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{The disambiguation reading fails.}  We priced every ordered
goal triple $(A, B, C)$ under three switching-cost models: a one-off bit
per switch; the conjecture's literal form (per-decision membership
entropy, accumulated over the support overlap, weighted by visitation);
and a sharper per-state variant that charges only where the two segment
policies disagree.  None restores the triangle inequality: on
\code{four\_rooms} the literal form covers only $17$--$88\%$ of
violating triples (worse as $\beta$ rises), the per-state variant
$3$--$32\%$, and the one-off bit misses the entire tail.  Decisively,
the \emph{worst} violations sit on triples whose segments barely overlap
($\sim 0.01$ expected shared visits, against a median of $1$--$2$ on
non-violating triples), and the gap is uncorrelated with every
identification cost ($|r| < 0.12$).  Overlap is not the source of the
breakage.

\paragraph{The violation is prior specialisation.}  Reprice leg one
under the direct problem's own converged marginal --- solve the
$A \to B$ leg with the reference held at $\hat{p}_C$ instead of
$\hat{p}_B$ --- and every violation collapses: zero triples above solver
noise, across environments, $\beta \in \{0.3, 1, 3\}$, and all six
labelling frames tested below (Figure~\ref{fig:decomposition}).
Tightening the solvers by three orders of magnitude leaves a maximum
residual of $5 \times 10^{-4}$ bits against gaps of $1$--$11$ bits, the
remainder attributable to the marginal-extraction systematic rather
than convergence.  The entire triangle violation is the
\emph{prior-specialisation credit}: what a leg saves by abandoning the
journey's action marginal for its own.  Segmentation pays where route
pieces want \emph{different marginals}, not where trajectories overlap.

  \caption[Decomposing the triangle violation]{The stopover discount is

  cheaper-via-a-waypoint route, the discount (gap) never exceeds what

\paragraph{The unstated assumption in the disjoint-support theorem.}
The supplementary theorem of \citet{Archer2022-space-of-goals} states
that essentially disjoint supports imply the triangle inequality.  Its
proof glues the two subpolicies into one and splits the glued free
energy into the two legs' free energies --- but in that expansion every
log term is written against a \emph{single} marginal $\hat{p}(a)$: the
same symbol in the leg-one terms, the leg-two terms, and the glued
policy's cost.  The split is an identity only when all three quantities
are referenced to one common prior, whereas the theorem's right-hand
side is defined with each leg's own self-consistent marginal
($\hat{p}_1 \neq \hat{p}_2$ in general).  Disjointness is needed only so
the glued policy is well defined; the load-bearing assumption is the
shared prior.  Made explicit, the proof establishes:

\begin{theorem}[common-prior glueing; \citealp{Archer2022-space-of-goals},
assumption made explicit]
\label{thm:common-prior}
If the supports of $\pi_1$ (goal $s'$, from $s$) and $\pi_2$ (goal $g$,
from $s'$) intersect only at $s'$, then for every state-independent
reference $q$,
$\Fen^{q}_g(s) \leq \Fen^{\pi_1, q}_{s'}(s) + \Fen^{\pi_2, q}_g(s')$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proposition}[conjectured: the disjointness condition is
unnecessary]
\label{prop:no-disjointness}
With a common reference $q$, the free energy is a quasi-metric with no
condition on support overlap.  For deterministic dynamics this is exact
(path concatenation makes $2^{-\beta \Fen^{q}}$ superadditive); for
stochastic dynamics we verify it empirically to below $10^{-3}$ bits on
$59{,}280$ triples at solver tolerance $10^{-8}$, and a proof is open.
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[the relaxed triangle inequality, resolved]
\label{cor:relaxed}
With per-leg self-consistent marginals --- the quantities the geometry
actually uses ---
\[
  \Fen_g(s) \;\leq\; \Fen_{s'}(s) + \Fen_g(s') + C_{\mathrm{switch}},
  \qquad
  C_{\mathrm{switch}} \;=\; \min_q\, [\, \Delta_1(q) + \Delta_2(q) \,]
  \;=\; \tfrac{n_1 + n_2}{\beta}\,
  \mathrm{JS}_w\!\left( \rho_1, \rho_2 \right),
\]
with $w = n_1 / (n_1 + n_2)$ and $\mathrm{JS}_w$ the weighted
Jensen--Shannon divergence, since the $q$ minimising the summed
swap costs \eqref{eq:prior-swap} is the visit-weighted mixture of the
two legs' marginals.
\end{corollary}

\paragraph{Reading the closed form.}  $C_{\mathrm{switch}}$ vanishes iff
$\rho_1 = \rho_2$ --- marginal agreement, not support disjointness: if
both legs move the same way, there is nothing to keep track of and
nothing to pay, however much their paths overlap.  It equals
$I(M; A)$, the mutual information between the section variable
$M$ (which leg) and the action: the switching cost is the part of
``which leg am I on'' that is \emph{visible in the actions} --- you pay
for tracking the leg only to the extent that your moves would give it
away.  And it is bounded by $H(M) = H_b(w)$, at most a coin-flip's
worth of information per expected step --- so the disambiguation
entropy of the original conjecture survives as the exact upper envelope
of the true term: the right object, at the level of action marginals
rather than per-state membership.  In our implementation the live distribution is
uniform, so marginals are goal-indexed and the choice $q = \hat{p}_g$
zeroes $\Delta_2$ exactly, giving the one-sided working bound
$\mathrm{gap} \leq \Delta_1(\hat{p}_g)$.  This bound held on
$100.0\%$ of $\sim\!350{,}000$ ordered triples across six labelling
frames, predicting the measured credit at $r = 0.995$ (Cartesian) and
$r = 0.93$--$0.96$ (evolved twists).

\paragraph{The twisted geometry, repriced.}  Section
\ref{sec:segmentation} showed the exemplar twist amplifies triangle
violations while cheapening the mean route; the corrected theory says
the amplification must be carried by the per-goal marginals, and it is
(Figure~\ref{fig:twist-ps}).  Across a cohort of five evolved twists
spanning dominant-label coverage $0.38$--$0.85$: pairwise marginal
divergence rises from a Cartesian median of $0.79$ bits to
$1.24$--$4.44$; the prior-specialisation credit separates cleanly along
the habit flow --- goal pairs whose marginals both load the dominant
label carry a median credit of $0.6$--$1.9$ bits-equivalent against
$6.1$--$15.9$ off-flow, a $4$--$8\times$ split in every twist, with
$\mathrm{corr}(\text{marginal KL}, \text{credit}) = 0.89$--$0.93$.  The
home-cycle flow is a \emph{marginal-compatibility class}: legs that
ride it segment for free, and the twist's extra violations live on the
class boundary.  Violation \emph{rate} and marginal \emph{divergence}
measure different things --- the saturated twists violate most often
($3.3\%$/$2.7\%$ of triples against $1.2$--$1.8\%$ for the fragmented
ones), while the fragmented twists have the most divergent marginals: a
coherent flow makes two-leg routes profitable \emph{often}; divergent
vocabularies make each one \emph{large}.  This is
Section~\ref{sec:segmentation}'s ``bent, not flattened'' made
quantitative: what evolution bends is the marginal-compatibility
structure of the space of goals.
