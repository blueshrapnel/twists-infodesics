\begin{abstract}
% DRAFT — one paragraph, to be rewritten once results are in.
A twist relabels an agent's actions per state.  Optimised twists build
\emph{habit cycles}: attractors that a single repeated action label drains
the environment into.  A companion paper characterises these structures
empirically; this paper explains them.  We show that a repeated-label flow
is a pure \emph{infodesic} in the space-of-goals geometry --- a route
followable with no state information --- and that the asymmetric homing
mode is a \emph{cost infodesic} segmented at informationally salient
waypoints: the free-energy triangle inequality fails across goal-specific
policies, policy switching becomes advantageous, and the doorway states at
which switches occur are exactly where the evolved label vocabulary changes
hands.  Curvature, concentrated at walls, is the knob: where the world is
flat the pure infodesic, the value geodesic, and the cost infodesic
coincide and homing saturates; where walls concentrate curvature the desics
split, routes segment into single-label legs, and the home-plus-escape
vocabulary of the companion paper emerges.  Switching costs quantify the
segmentation: the cost of abandoning a habit decomposes into an ambient
term set by the environment's drift and a dominant policy-specific term set
by the habit being abandoned.
\end{abstract}

\section{Background: the free-energy geometry and its desics}
\label{sec:background}

% TODO: reconcile notation with cognitive-geometry.tex §Infodesics before
% submission; definitions below are the working forms.

For an informationally bounded agent, the cost of reaching a goal $g$ from
a state $s$ is the free energy of the goal-optimal policy,
$\Fen_g(s) = -V^{\pi^*}(s) + \beta^{-1} \ItgD^{\pi^*}(s)$: expected path
cost plus the decision information the route demands, weighed by the
agent's parsimony $\beta$.  Collecting these values over all ordered pairs
gives a cost matrix $D[s, g] = \Fen_g(s)$ --- a quasi-metric on the world:
non-negative, zero on the diagonal, and in general \emph{asymmetric},
because moving with a prevailing structure costs less information than
moving against it.

Routes through this geometry come in kinds.  A \emph{pure infodesic} is a
route followable with no state information at all: action selection
independent of state, $\ItgD \to 0$ --- following the same command
everywhere, the informational analogue of a loxodrome's constant bearing.
A \emph{value geodesic} is a shortest route in the expected-cost sense
alone.  A \emph{cost infodesic} is a route optimal in the full free
energy; on such a route through an intermediate $B$, the triangle
inequality
\[
  D[A, C] \;\leq\; D[A, B] + D[B, C]
\]
holds with equality.  Where the triangle inequality \emph{fails} ---
$D[A, B] + D[B, C] < D[A, C]$ --- reaching $C$ via $B$, abandoning the
goal-$C$ policy at $B$ for a fresh one, is strictly cheaper than the
direct route: multi-policy processing beats single-policy processing, and
the optimal behaviour \emph{segments} at $B$.  The states at which such
segmentation is profitable are the geometry's informationally salient
waypoints.

On a flat, translation-invariant world these three notions collapse: one
constant command is also shortest and also free-energy-optimal, and no
segmentation ever pays.  Where structure --- walls, doorways, seams ---
concentrates curvature, the desics separate, and the question of this
paper is what evolution does about it when it is allowed to redesign the
action labels themselves.

\section{Single-label flows are pure infodesics}
\label{sec:pure}

A pure infodesic is a sequence visitable with no state information ---
action selection independent of state, decision information
$\ItgD \to 0$: following the same direction with as little cognitive
correction as possible.  A single-label flow is exactly that.  Press one
label in every state: the label is state-independent even though the twist
makes the \emph{physical} direction vary cell to cell.  The home vector of
the companion paper is the direction field of a pure infodesic; the habit
cycle is the attractor the pure infodesic drains into --- its terminus.
The habit is the whole pure-infodesic object: flow plus limit set.

This identification does real work.  It explains why decision-information
descent builds habit cycles at all: the fitness rewards routes that
approach pure infodesics, and under a twist the set of realisable pure
infodesics is exactly the set of single-label flows --- so evolution
shapes $\sigma$ until some label's flow is worth following from almost
anywhere.  It also assigns the correct formal role to the attractor: the
cycle is not a defect of the flow but its goal, the recurrent set the
open-loop policy is \emph{for}.

\section{The asymmetric mode is a segmented cost infodesic}
\label{sec:segmentation}

\paragraph{Segmentation is a walled-world phenomenon.}  We measured the
triangle inequality systematically: for each environment and
$\beta \in \{0.3, 1, 3\}$, every ordered goal triple $(A, B, C)$ of the
Cartesian world, a violation being a strictly cheaper segmented route
(Figure~\ref{fig:triangle}).  The dichotomy is clean.  Flat worlds ---
the torus and the bounded open grid --- produce \emph{zero} violating
triples at every $\beta$: the maximum gap is strictly negative, no policy
switch ever pays, and the desics coincide as Section~\ref{sec:background}
predicts.  Walled worlds violate: on \code{four\_rooms} one in six
ordered pairs has a profitable midpoint; the corridor ring violates on
$3$--$4\%$ of triples.  The gap is informational in origin: its maximum
grows as $\beta$ falls ($1.1 \to 3.4 \to 11.0$ bits-equivalent across
$\beta = 3 \to 1 \to 0.3$ on \code{four\_rooms}) --- value alone
satisfies the triangle inequality, and it is the information cost of one
policy serving a two-legged route that makes segmentation pay, amplified
by parsimony.

  \caption[Triangle-inequality violation across the palette]{Triangle
  violations in the Cartesian free-energy geometry.  \emph{Left:}
  violating fraction of ordered goal triples by environment and $\beta$

  distributions at $\beta = 1$ (positive = segmenting is cheaper).

\paragraph{The waypoints are the doorway flanks --- and behaviour
agrees.}  The profitable midpoints do not sit \emph{on} the doorway
cells but one step past them.  The behavioural face of the same fact:
rolling greedy routes for every start--goal pair and recording where the
issued label changes, the switch rate per visit is exactly zero at the
doorway cells under the Cartesian labelling and maximal on their flanks
($0.46$ against $0.38$ elsewhere) --- the flow passes \emph{through} a
door; the decision happens after crossing (Figure~\ref{fig:switch}).
Geometry predicts behaviour: per-state switch events correlate with
profitable-midpoint counts at $r = 0.72$.  The flat control switches
\emph{more} overall ($0.47$ per step against $0.37$) but with no spatial
structure: plenty of switching, no waypoints.  Segmentation, on both the
geometric and the behavioural ledger, is what walls make.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{F-segmentation-switch-points.png}
  \caption[Switch points along greedy routes]{Label-switch locations
  along greedy routes (switch rate per visit; doorways outlined).
  Cartesian \code{four\_rooms}, the evolved exemplar twist, and the flat
  control; right, geometry against behaviour.  Doors are corridors, not
  decision points; the twist needs $40\%$ fewer switches; the flat world

  \label{fig:switch}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{What the twist does to the geometry --- the refuted
prediction.}  If the evolved twist internalises route structure, one
might expect the twisted world's geometry to offer \emph{fewer}
profitable segmentation points.  The opposite holds
(Figure~\ref{fig:twist-triangle}): on the exemplar twist the violating
pairs multiply from $258$ to $610$ of $1{,}560$, at every $\beta$, with
larger gaps --- while the mean direct cost falls.  The twist does not
flatten the cost geometry; it \emph{curves it further}.  The labelling
installs a strong drift field --- the homing flow --- and a directional
geometry is precisely one in which two-leg routes, riding one flow and
then another, undercut direct ones.  There is no tension with the
behavioural switch reduction above: the optimal policies issue fewer
switch \emph{events} because each label now serves a longer leg, while
the geometry affords more profitable re-planning points because the
flows are stronger.  Behaviour segments less; geometry affords more.
Evolution does not straighten the free-energy geometry --- it bends it
so that its own habit legs are the cheap segments, which is what it
means, in geometric terms, for the representation to have absorbed the
segmentation.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering

\end{figure}
